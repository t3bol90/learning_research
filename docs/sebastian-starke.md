# A model PhD student: Sebastian Starke

> Document index (GitHub repo): [https://github.com/pengsida/learning_research](https://github.com/pengsida/learning_research)

Over the last couple of days I read the series of papers Sebastian Starke worked on during his PhD on Character Control, and I learned a lot ([paper notes (Notion)](https://www.notion.so/1703fe292ff1809e92d2ff48f47e06de) (Notion)). I do not know Sebastian Starke personally, but just from reading his papers, I have a lot of admiration and many takeaways. I am writing them down here so I can come back and reflect later.

[Sebastian Starke](https://github.com/sebastianstarke) did his PhD in Taku Komura's group. He published one paper at SIGGRAPH every year of his PhD, and across five years he built a Character Control system that is close to being production-ready. It is impressive, and it has lifted what is achievable for game animation systems in industry by a long way. On GitHub, his [AI4Animation](https://github.com/sebastianstarke/AI4Animation) repo has 7.4k stars. Academically, each of his SIGGRAPH papers steadily knocks down a harder industry problem, going deeper in one direction. The last paper of his PhD, DeepPhase, won the SIGGRAPH best paper award, and well deserved.

Reading Sebastian Starke's series of papers, you can see his research style:

1. **Very clear, long-term goal, problem-driven research.** His papers are always solving problems inside the Character Control system, going from early simple periodic motion control to later complex motion control, taking down one mountain after another in the Character Control direction.
2. **Always finds problems that are worth solving.** Probably benefiting from Taku's group's long-running accumulation, Sebastian's papers always find the highest-priority problems in Character Control, rather than spending energy on side issues that do not really matter or are not pressing.
3. **Not afraid of hard problems and willing to fill in the gaps, taking on questions that have "no answer".** What sets Sebastian apart is the willingness to take on hard problems and keep filling in the gaps. One of the central problems in Character Control is the complexity of the target motion. His senior labmate Daniel Holden's PFNN solved simple periodic motion. Sebastian's Local Motion Phase pushed it further and solved compositional motion. Then DeepPhase (SIGGRAPH best paper) solved complex non-periodic motion, which essentially closes out the motion-complexity problem.

   Compared with "target motion complexity", there are other problems in Character Control that are easier to publish on, such as Multi-modal Control. Sebastian did not go after those just because they are easier to publish. He kept his energy on the most important problems, the kind that do not have a well-established solution. His willingness to take on hard problems and keep filling gaps is the key to why he could push the industry forward.
4. **Very strong technical ability.** The first three points let Sebastian focus his energy on solving the most important problems, but actually being able to solve them comes down to his very strong technical ability: broad technical background, deep technical thinking, and hardcore engineering. Sebastian appears to have worked through the whole Character Control technology stack, including Motion Capture, Fitting, Animation, Deep Learning, Unity, and so on.
5. **Tries new tech early.** Sebastian's papers always try the frontier techniques of the time. For example, DeepPhase explored the use of Representation Learning in motion. I believe that if he had simply followed the technical path of Local Motion Phase to solve complex non-periodic motion control, the gain would not have been as large.

After carefully studying and summarising Sebastian Starke's research style, I reflected on the mistakes I made during my own PhD. Some of these mistakes also show up occasionally in students I supervise, which means their cleverness ends up being spent in the wrong place. The specific mistakes are:

1. **No long-term goal, not focused on solving problems in one direction.** During my PhD, in the first three years I switched topics three times: Object Pose Estimation, Instance Segmentation, Dynamic 3D Reconstruction. Luckily I eventually settled on Dynamic 3D Reconstruction. If I had focused on Object Pose Estimation from the start, my PhD would have gone more smoothly, I could have done better work in Object Pose, and I would have had my own label earlier on.
2. **Tendency to solve easy problems instead of looking for the most valuable ones.** In my third year, I thought Dynamic Human Surface Reconstruction was an easy problem and an easy paper to write, so I spent half a year on the Animatable SDF project. Looking back, those six months were a complete waste, I did not learn anything new. If I had spent that time on a valuable problem, like improving the rendering quality of 3D human bodies, my technical skills would have grown more, and I would have had a better shot at making a bigger impact in Dynamic 3D Reconstruction.
3. **Lack of awareness of the need to push a problem to 99% solved.** After Neural Body, I had brought rendering quality up to a watchable level. At the time I was chasing the Fancy and immediately moved on to Animatable Human. In those two years, people from industry kept telling me that dynamic human rendering quality urgently needed to improve, but I did not go and solve it. Looking back, in dynamic human reconstruction I just published a few papers but did not really solve any problem, and I became "a person who only publishes papers and contributes nothing real to industry".
4. **Wasting time on problems that already have answers.** In my second year I did Deep Snake. The problem it tried to solve had already been handled fairly well by an earlier paper, Curve-GCN, and I was just patching things on top. Looking back it feels meaningless, and at the time I was full of self-doubt while doing it. Projects like that just burn out your research enthusiasm. There is only downside, no upside.
5. **Narrow paper reading and thin technical background.** I did not have this problem myself, but I have seen students who do not have the habit of reading widely, which is a real shame.
6. **Not trying new tech early.** During my PhD, several new techniques were emerging, such as Transformer, Diffusion Model, and Video Model. My thinking back then was strange. I always felt new techniques were just hype, which is very Qing-dynasty thinking, and I spent a lot of time on small fixes within the existing technical framework. If I had instead treated new tech as a source of hope and asked whether it could solve hard problems in my own area, I might have done better work. PVNet and Neural Body are actually examples of using new tech well, but at the time I had no real awareness of why they were strong. I could only do those two works because Professor Zhou guided me well, gave me good problems, and was firm that I should not use the older technical approach.

I am writing this to summarise where Sebastian succeeded, and to reflect on many mistakes I made. I hope I can keep learning from the strong people, put my energy on important problems, and always try to be on the front line of new tech.
