# Getting help from senior researchers more effectively

> **Why this document exists**<br>From the student's side: when some students hit a wall in their research, they don't think to ask a senior researcher for help, or they are afraid or unsure how to describe the problem clearly. So they end up trying to solve everything alone.<br>From the senior researcher's side: a student's research project moves slowly. Every time the senior asks what the issues are, the student can't lay them out clearly. It feels like punching cotton: the senior wants to help the project but has nothing to push against.

<summary>Negative example</summary>

One student ran into trouble with data processing during a project but did not feel comfortable telling their advisor exactly what was going wrong. They tried to figure it out on their own.

In every meeting with the advisor, they only reported progress and **never raised concrete questions**. So the advisor could only give rough suggestions based on the progress and could **not directly solve the student's actual problems**.

The result was a project that crawled along, with both the student and the advisor frustrated.

</details>

Basic principles for getting help from a senior researcher more effectively:

1. When you hit a problem, sort out the issue and your own thinking, then go to the senior researcher promptly. Do not put it off.
2. Prepare proper meeting slides: [How to run a meeting](./weekly-meeting-slides.md)
3. Sort out your overall thinking on the project so you can explain it clearly to the senior researcher.
4. Sort out the problems you have hit so you can explain them clearly to the senior researcher.
5. Keep the senior researcher in the loop on the project day to day.
6. Let the senior researcher know your current research ability so they can assign tasks that fit. Do not oversell yourself. If something feels too hard, say so plainly: admit that the current task is beyond you and ask them to give you work that matches your level.
