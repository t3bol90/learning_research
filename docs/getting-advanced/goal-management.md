# The role of goal management in research and life

<!-- zh: 段永平：我给大家举一个小例子，它对我一生的影响非常大。 -->
Duan Yongping: Let me give a small example that had a huge effect on the rest of my life. I graduated from high school in 1977, right after the college entrance exam was reinstated. Getting into university back then was extremely hard, and once you were in, you were treated as one of the chosen few. So for me, getting into university was the biggest goal I had, and I kept pushing toward it. But my foundation was very weak. I sat the exam twice. The first time, my four subjects added up to a little over eighty points in total, which was nowhere near enough to get in. My second attempt came less than half a year after the first, with Spring Festival in between. I remember that during that period, to prepare for the exam I had to learn the entire middle-school maths, physics and chemistry curriculum, memorise the political theory, and basically not review Chinese at all, since I felt there was no way to revise Chinese and no idea where to start. In the end I did very well, scoring over four hundred points, an average of more than eighty per subject. I was excited the whole time. But when the admission letter actually arrived, I started to feel lost. It hit me that all that hard work had been for a single piece of paper, and that feeling stayed with me for several years. After thinking about it for a long time, it suddenly clicked: the process of chasing a goal is the part that matters most, and once you have the thing in hand it stops feeling meaningful, with the exception of love. So no matter what the goal is, you go all in while you are chasing it, but the moment you get it you may immediately lose interest.

<!-- zh: 赚钱也是一样，其实赚钱的乐趣是非常小的 -->
Making money is the same. The pleasure of making money is actually very small. You will not know that until you have made some, but once you have, you understand what I mean. There is nothing wrong with making money, but if you make money for the sake of making money, that is a problem. So no matter how much wealth I own, the wealth itself has not given me anything in particular. What matters is whether I can reach the goals I care about. My current goals are very simple. I want to do my investing well, and most of my energy now goes into raising my kids. In China there are probably very few business fathers who spend more time on their children than I do. When I am back in China, anyone who calls me on the weekend gets an earful. Weekends are for playing with my children and being with my family. I find this fun, and I am happy seeing my kids grow up every day. I have no special expectations of them either. Whether they take over the family business in the future is none of my concern. What they want to do is up to them. If they are happy, I am happy.

<!-- zh: 我住的地方离斯坦福大学很近 -->
I live close to Stanford, so I plan to go back to school next year and study non-profit organisations. Right now everyone is interested in for-profit organisations, but I want to learn how to set up a non-profit. A non-profit is not the same as not making money. The money it earns goes toward the things it wants to do. A non-profit hospital, for example, still has to make money, but the return on investment is very low. How to keep a non-profit running well is something I am not very clear about, because in China this concept never really existed, or at least I have never come across it. Now I have many goals, every day feels full and happy, and that is what I learned from the college entrance exam. No matter what, you have to have a goal. You have to know what you want to do, and when you get it done, you will feel happy.
