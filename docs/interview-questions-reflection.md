# Reflecting on research from interview questions

> Document index (GitHub repo): [https://github.com/pengsida/learning_research](https://github.com/pengsida/learning_research)

The notes below are based on Yuanming Hu's article ([https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/1893638374646079902](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/1893638374646079902)).

The questions below are ones that Yuanming Hu raised during the Meshy scholarship defence.

## The core question

The core question: how do you take research from the "fundamentals stage" to the "impact stage"?

- "Fundamentals stage": after going through this stage, you have a rough grasp of the basics of academia, such as doing surveys, coming up with ideas, running experiments, writing papers, and so on. At this stage, you can roughly produce a publication every time you have one paper-worthy idea.
- "Impact stage": just publishing a paper no longer satisfies you. You want to produce work that has real impact.

## Question 1: the meaning of the project

Q: "If what you are working on is not important, and is unlikely to lead to important things, why are you working on it?"

Einstein once said: If I had an hour to solve a problem I'd spend 55 minutes thinking about the problem and 5 minutes thinking about solutions.

Q: "The direction you are working in hasn't seen new progress for years, mainly because the field stopped caring about the problem you are solving long ago. The strongest people have all moved on to other things, so there isn't much room left to make an impact. You're aware of this, right?"
A: "I try not to think about it. I just want to get the paper out."
Q: "Does a paper that no one cares about still need to be published?"

> Reflection: in research, we should spend more time finding the right problem.

## Question 2: on taking risks

Q: "The idea you mentioned just now is interesting and could have a big impact. Why aren't you doing it?"
A: "I want to think it over a bit more. Once I'm clearer, I'll start. Besides, there are still some low-hanging fruit on my other directions. I can squeeze out a couple more SIGGRAPH papers from those."

> Reflection: in research, aim high and have the courage to act.

## Question 3: change

Q: "Have you considered switching to a different problem?"
A: "I'm worried that if I switch, I won't be familiar with the new problem and won't be able to publish papers for a while..."
Q: "But in the long run, doing work with greater impact is probably better for you, even if you give up the problem you're already comfortable with. Besides, your previous knowledge isn't completely useless, you're just extending the problem."

> Reflection: in research, be willing to embrace change.

## Question 4: solving problems at their root

Q: "Isn't this just combining the x from work A with the y from work B? And the whole system is more complicated, but the results aren't fundamentally better. What do you think an ideal paper looks like? How would you achieve that?"
A: "Good question. I've never thought about it..."

> Reflection: in research, solve problems from first principles. Patching things up can solve some problems in the short term, but it will be replaced sooner or later.

## Question 5: on collaboration

Q: "Who does the best research in your area? Have you talked to them? Why are they so good at it? Could you be like that too?"
A: "I'm a bit shy. I'm afraid of bothering others, and I'm afraid of being bothered. I just want to focus on my own world."

> Reflection: have the courage to work with people who are stronger than you.

## Question 6: independent thinking

Q: "Does your professor study this problem every day the way you do? Is what they say always right? When I was at MIT, many professors said their best students never listened to them, but they came up with better ways to solve the problem."
Q: "Those are all things other people said. What do you think yourself?"

> Reflection: in research, think independently and work out your own approach.
